Abstract

Accurate recognition of chessboard states from images is a significant challenge in computer vision, primarily due to the difficulty of obtaining large-scale labeled real-world datasets. In this project, we address the Synthetic-to-Real domain gap by leveraging photorealistic synthetic data generated in Blender. We formulate the task as a per-square classification problem using a ResNet-18 architecture. The primary objective of this work is to develop and evaluate a pipeline that effectively transfers knowledge from a simulated environment to real-world chessboard images, exploring various domain adaptation strategies to overcome the inherent visual differences between the two domains.

Methodology

Our pipeline consists of four modular stages designed to normalize the input before classification:


Figure 1: Project Overview. The system generalizes from Blender-generated synthetic boards (left) to predicted boardstate visualization (right) using a ResNet-18 per-square classifier.

  • Synthetic Generation: Using Blender to render high-resolution (1024x1024) chessboards from FEN strings with randomized lighting and angles.
  • Preprocessing: Detecting the board and applying perspective transformation to obtain a canonical 512x512 top-down view.
  • Square Extraction: Cropping individual 8x8 squares with context-aware margins.
  • Classification: A ResNet-18 model predicts one of 13 classes (12 pieces + empty) for each square.

Results & Comparison

To assess real-world performance, we evaluated our model on a fully labeled dataset of real chessboard images. As shown in the table below, while Fine-tuning on real data yielded the highest raw Square Accuracy, our Mixed Training strategy proved to be the most balanced, achieving the highest Macro F1-score and significantly outperforming the Zero-shot baseline.
